# 🍎 Nutrition and Hydration

Welcome to the **Nutrition and Hydration** section of the 5K Race Preparation Knowledge Base.

Running performance depends on more than workouts alone. Food and fluids help provide energy for training, support recovery afterward, and prepare runners for future sessions.

A runner does not need a complicated diet to prepare for a 5K. The goal is to develop consistent habits that support training without making nutrition unnecessarily difficult.

> Good nutrition and hydration help support the training that produces better running performance.

## 🍽️ Build Balanced Meals

A balanced meal for a runner may include:

- A carbohydrate source
- A protein source
- Fruits or vegetables
- A source of healthy fats
- Fluids

For example:

| Meal | Example |
|---|---|
| Breakfast | Oatmeal, banana, eggs, and milk |
| Lunch | Chicken, rice, vegetables, and fruit |
| Dinner | Pasta, lean meat, vegetables, and water |
| Snack | Greek yogurt with fruit and granola |

The exact foods can change based on preference, budget, culture, and availability.

The important goal is consistently eating enough to support training.

Learn more in [[6.nutrition-and-hydration/fueling-for-running|Fueling for Running]].

## ⚡ Fuel Harder Training

Different workouts may require different levels of preparation.

An easy 20-minute run may not require the same amount of planning as a longer run or difficult speed session.

Before demanding workouts such as:

- [[3.speed-workouts/interval-training|Interval Training]]
- [[3.speed-workouts/tempo-runs|Tempo Runs]]
- [[2.weekly-training-plan/long-run-day|Long Run Day]]

runners may benefit from eating a carbohydrate-containing meal or snack beforehand.

Simple examples include:

- Banana
- Toast with peanut butter
- Oatmeal
- Granola bar
- Fruit with yogurt

The amount and timing depend on the runner and the workout.

## 💧 Daily Hydration

Hydration should not begin only when the workout starts.

Runners should drink fluids throughout the day and pay attention to conditions that increase fluid loss.

Hydration needs may increase because of:

- Hot weather
- Humidity
- Longer runs
- Heavy sweating
- Multiple training sessions

Water may be sufficient for many normal training sessions.

Longer or hotter workouts may require greater attention to fluids and electrolytes.

Visit [[6.nutrition-and-hydration/hydration-for-runners|Hydration for Runners]] for practical examples.

## 🏃 Example Training-Day Nutrition

A simple training day might look like this:

**Breakfast**

Oatmeal with banana and eggs

**Lunch**

Chicken, rice, vegetables, and fruit

**Pre-Run Snack**

Banana or toast approximately 1 to 2 hours before training

**Training**

Easy run or speed workout

**Post-Run**

Greek yogurt with fruit or a balanced meal containing carbohydrates and protein

**Dinner**

Pasta or rice with a protein source and vegetables

This is only an example.

Runners should adjust meal size and timing based on their training schedule and individual preferences.

## 🔄 Nutrition and Recovery

After harder training, runners should focus on both fluids and food.

A simple post-workout recovery approach might be:

1. Drink water after finishing.
2. Cool down for 5 to 10 minutes.
3. Eat a snack or meal containing carbohydrates and protein.
4. Continue drinking fluids throughout the day.
5. Eat normal balanced meals afterward.

Examples of recovery foods include:

- Chocolate milk
- Greek yogurt and fruit
- Eggs with toast
- Chicken and rice
- Turkey sandwich
- Smoothie with fruit and yogurt

This process connects directly with [[5.recovery-and-injury-prevention/post-run-recovery|Post-Run Recovery]].

## 📅 Nutrition Across the Training Week

Nutrition should support the complete weekly schedule.

| Training Day | Main Nutrition Focus |
|---|---|
| Easy Run | Normal balanced meals and hydration |
| Speed Workout | Carbohydrates before training and recovery meal afterward |
| Strength Day | Adequate protein and overall energy |
| Long Run | Pre-run fueling and additional hydration attention |
| Recovery Day | Balanced meals and continued hydration |

Runners do not need a completely different diet every day.

Instead, meal size and timing can be adjusted based on how demanding the training session will be.

---

## ⚠️ Avoid Major Changes on Race Day

Race morning is not the best time to experiment with unfamiliar foods or drinks.

Runners should practice their pre-run nutrition during training before using the same approach on race day.

For example, if oatmeal and a banana work well before a long run, the runner may choose a similar familiar meal before the race.

The goal is to arrive at the starting line:

- Properly fueled
- Hydrated
- Comfortable
- Familiar with the routine

Learn more in [[7.race-day-strategy/race-morning-preparation|Race Morning Preparation]].
