# Core Training for Running

Core training can support a runner's ability to maintain posture, control movement, and stay stable while running. The core includes more than just the abdominal muscles. It also involves muscles around the trunk, lower back, hips, and pelvis that help the upper and lower body work together.

For someone preparing for a 5K, core training does not need to involve hundreds of sit-ups or extremely long workouts. A small amount of consistent core work can complement running and [[4.strength-and-mobility/strength-training-for-runners|Strength Training for Runners]].

## Why Core Strength Matters

Every running stride requires the body to transfer force while maintaining control. As a runner becomes tired, posture may begin to change and unnecessary movement may increase.

A stronger and more stable core can help the runner maintain better body control during longer or harder efforts.

Core strength may be especially useful during:

- [[2.weekly-training-plan/long-run-day|Long Run Days]]
    
- [[3.speed-workouts/interval-training|Interval Training]]
    
- [[3.speed-workouts/hill-repeats|Hill Repeats]]
    
- The later stages of a 5K race
    

This also connects to [[1.running-basics/proper-running-form|Proper Running Form]], because posture and stability become more difficult to maintain as fatigue increases.

## Useful Core Exercises

Core training can remain simple and still be effective.

Common exercises include:

1. Front planks
    
2. Side planks
    
3. Dead bugs
    
4. Bird dogs
    
5. Pallof presses
    
6. Farmer carries
    
7. Glute bridges
    

These exercises challenge the body in different ways.

For example, a plank requires the runner to resist excessive movement, while a side plank places more emphasis on lateral stability. Dead bugs and bird dogs require controlled movement while maintaining trunk position.

### Example Core Routine

A simple routine might include:

- Front plank: 2 to 3 sets
    
- Side plank: 2 sets per side
    
- Dead bugs: 8 to 12 controlled repetitions
    
- Bird dogs: 8 to 12 repetitions per side
    
- Glute bridges: 10 to 15 repetitions
    

The exact number of repetitions or time should depend on the runner's experience.

> Core training should focus on control and quality rather than simply holding a position for as long as possible.

## Quality Over Duration

One common mistake is assuming that a longer plank automatically means a better workout.

If posture begins to break down, continuing for additional time may provide less benefit than completing a shorter set with better control.

During core exercises, runners should focus on:

- Maintaining steady breathing
    
- Avoiding excessive arching of the lower back
    
- Keeping movements slow and controlled
    
- Maintaining stable posture
    
- Stopping when technique begins to deteriorate
    

The same principle applies to running. Good movement quality is usually more useful than forcing extra repetitions with poor technique.

## How Often Should Runners Train the Core?

Core exercises can be included one to three times per week depending on the runner's overall training schedule.

They can be added to:

- Strength training sessions
    
- Easy running days
    
- Short mobility routines
    
- Recovery-focused sessions
    

Core training does not need to become a separate hour-long workout.

A short routine performed consistently may be easier to maintain than an extremely demanding program.

## Core Training and Fatigue

The core also experiences fatigue, which means it should be considered as part of the overall training plan.

A very demanding core session immediately before an important race or hard workout may not be necessary.

Instead, core work should fit naturally into the [[2.weekly-training-plan/index|Weekly Training Plan]].

The goal is to strengthen the body without creating so much additional fatigue that running quality suffers.

## Connecting Core Strength to 5K Performance

During a 5K, fatigue often increases during the final portion of the race. A runner may begin to tighten the shoulders, lose posture, or allow unnecessary movement.

Core strength alone will not guarantee perfect running form, but it can support the ability to remain controlled when the body becomes tired.

This is especially important when trying to follow a strong [[7.race-day-strategy/finishing-the-5k-strong|Finishing the 5K Strong]] strategy.

Core training works best when combined with [[4.strength-and-mobility/mobility-and-flexibility|Mobility and Flexibility]], general strength training, and consistent running.

Together, these elements help create a stronger and more balanced approach to 5K preparation.
