# 🧱 Core Training for Running

Core training can help runners develop better stability, posture, and body control.

The core includes more than the abdominal muscles. It also involves muscles surrounding the trunk, hips, and lower back that help connect movement between the upper and lower body.

For runners, core training should focus on control and stability rather than completing hundreds of repetitions.

> A strong core helps runners maintain controlled movement as fatigue increases during training and racing.

---

## 🧠 Why Core Strength Matters

During running, the arms and legs are constantly moving while the trunk helps stabilize the body.

Core strength can support:

- Running posture
- Balance
- Body control
- Stability during fatigue
- Coordination between the upper and lower body

Core training also connects directly with [[1.running-basics/proper-running-form|Proper Running Form]].

As runners become tired, posture may begin to change. Developing core strength can help support better control during longer or more demanding workouts.

---

## 🧱 Example Runner Core Workout

A basic core workout can be completed 2 to 3 times per week.

| Exercise | Sets | Reps or Time |
|---|---:|---:|
| Front Plank | 2–3 | 30–45 sec |
| Side Plank | 2 | 20–30 sec each side |
| Dead Bug | 2–3 | 8–10 each side |
| Bird Dog | 2 | 8–10 each side |
| Glute Bridge | 2–3 | 12–15 |
| Pallof Press | 2 | 10 each side |

Rest approximately 30 to 60 seconds between exercises when needed.

The entire routine can usually be completed in approximately 10 to 15 minutes.

---

## 🧍 Planks for Stability

Planks teach the core to resist unnecessary movement.

During a front plank:

1. Keep the body in a straight position.
2. Keep the hips from dropping.
3. Avoid excessively arching the lower back.
4. Maintain normal breathing.
5. Stop the set when good position can no longer be maintained.

A beginner might begin with:

**2 sets × 20 seconds**

Then progress toward:

**3 sets × 45 to 60 seconds**

Longer is not automatically better. Maintaining good position is more important than holding a plank with poor technique.

---

## 🔄 Dead Bugs and Bird Dogs

Dynamic core exercises train stability while the arms and legs move.

### Dead Bug

Complete:

**2 to 3 sets × 8 to 10 repetitions per side**

Focus on keeping the lower back controlled while slowly extending the opposite arm and leg.

### Bird Dog

Complete:

**2 sets × 8 to 10 repetitions per side**

Extend the opposite arm and leg while keeping the hips and trunk stable.

These exercises can be especially useful for runners because they require the body to maintain control while the limbs move.

---

## ↔️ Side Planks

Side planks focus on the muscles along the sides of the trunk and hips.

A simple progression is:

**Beginner**

2 sets × 15 to 20 seconds per side

**Intermediate**

2 to 3 sets × 30 seconds per side

**Advanced**

Add a leg lift or use another controlled variation.

Runners should avoid allowing the hips to drop as fatigue increases.

---

## 📅 Where Core Training Fits

Core training does not require its own dedicated training day.

It can be added after:

- Easy runs
- Strength workouts
- Short recovery sessions

An example schedule might be:

| Day | Training |
|---|---|
| Monday | Rest |
| Tuesday | Easy Run + Core |
| Wednesday | Speed Workout |
| Thursday | Easy Run |
| Friday | Strength + Core |
| Saturday | Long Run |
| Sunday | Recovery |

This allows runners to train the core without interfering with important running sessions.

## ⚡ Core Finisher Example

Runners who want a shorter routine can complete this circuit after an easy run:

1. Plank — 30 seconds
2. Side plank — 20 seconds each side
3. Dead bug — 8 repetitions each side
4. Bird dog — 8 repetitions each side

Rest for approximately 60 seconds.

Repeat for **2 to 3 total rounds**.

This creates a practical core session that can be completed without a large amount of equipment.

---

## 📈 How to Progress Core Training

Core exercises should gradually become more challenging as strength improves.

Progression may include:

- Increasing hold times
- Adding repetitions
- Adding another set
- Slowing down each repetition
- Adding light resistance
- Using more challenging variations

For example:

**Week 1:** Plank for 20 seconds

**Week 2:** Plank for 30 seconds

**Week 3:** Plank for 40 seconds

**Week 4:** Add another set or progress to a harder variation

The goal is better control rather than simply completing more repetitions.

---

## 🏃 Core Strength During Running

Core strength becomes especially useful when fatigue begins to increase.

During workouts such as:

- [[2.weekly-training-plan/long-run-day|Long Run Day]]
- [[3.speed-workouts/interval-training|Interval Training]]
- [[3.speed-workouts/tempo-runs|Tempo Runs]]

runners may notice posture becoming harder to maintain.

A strong and controlled trunk can help support better movement during these demanding portions of training.

Core strength should still be viewed as one part of the complete training system rather than a replacement for running.

---

## 💪 Combine Core and Strength Training

Core work can easily be included within a complete strength program.

For example, runners might complete:

**Lower Body**

Squat — 3 × 8–12

Romanian Deadlift — 3 × 8–10

Reverse Lunge — 2 × 8 each leg

**Core**

Plank — 2 × 30–45 sec

Dead Bug — 2 × 10 each side

Side Plank — 2 × 20–30 sec each side

This creates a balanced session that trains both lower-body strength and trunk stability.
