# Understanding Running Pace

Running pace describes how quickly a runner covers a specific distance, usually expressed as minutes per mile or minutes per kilometer. Understanding pace is important for 5K training because not every workout should be completed at the same speed or level of effort.

A runner who treats every training session like a race may accumulate unnecessary fatigue. On the other hand, a runner who never challenges their normal pace may miss opportunities to become more comfortable with faster running. Learning to match effort to the purpose of a workout creates a more balanced training approach.

## Easy Pace

An easy pace should feel controlled and sustainable. One practical method for judging an easy effort is the **talk test**. During an easy run, a runner should generally be able to speak in sentences without feeling completely out of breath.

Easy running is commonly used during [[2.weekly-training-plan/easy-run-days|Easy Run Days]] and for much of the [[2.weekly-training-plan/long-run-day|Long Run Day]].

The exact pace may change depending on factors such as:

- Weather
    
- Hills
    
- Fatigue
    
- Sleep
    
- Previous workouts
    
- Current fitness level
    

This is why effort can sometimes be more useful than trying to force the exact same numerical pace every day.

## Moderate and Faster Efforts

As effort increases, conversation becomes more difficult. A tempo-style effort is faster and more focused than an easy run but should still remain controlled. Interval workouts may involve even faster periods of running followed by recovery.

Different types of faster training are explained in:

### Why Pace Matters on Race Day

One of the biggest pacing challenges during a 5K is resisting the temptation to start much faster than a runner can maintain. Excitement, other competitors, and the race environment can make the opening minutes feel easier than expected.

Starting at a controlled effort can leave more energy available during the later portions of the race.

> The fastest possible first mile does not always produce the fastest possible 5K.

This concept is explored further in [[7.race-day-strategy/race-day-pacing|Race Day Pacing]].

Learning pace takes time. Watches and running apps can provide useful information, but runners should also learn to recognize effort through breathing, muscle fatigue, and overall comfort.
